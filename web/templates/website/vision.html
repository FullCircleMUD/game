The Founder's Vision

I grew up in the age of 28.8 and 56k modems, before World of Warcraft and the graphical MMOs took over. I invested — or wasted, depending on who you ask — a good portion of my misspent teenage years playing MUDs. Most notably on a derivative of CircleMUD, which was itself a derivative of DikuMUD.

Those games had something that the graphical successors never quite captured. They were raw, text-driven, imagination-first. The world lived in your head, not on your screen. And they were addictive in a way that came from depth, not dopamine loops.

Full Circle MUD aims to bring those old CircleMUD worlds full circle — combining the same addictive, compelling gameplay with ideas that simply weren't possible on a 56k modem in the late '90s.

{# ── Player-Driven Economy ── #}

A Player-Driven Economy

The vast majority of items in the game are harvested or crafted by players. If a shopkeeper sells it, a player gathered it or made it and sold it to the shopkeeper first.

Farmers harvest wheat. Millers grind it to flour. Bakers bake bread. Miners pull ore from the ground. Smelters refine ingots. Blacksmiths forge weapons. The economy isn't a backdrop — it's the backbone of the world, and players are every link in the supply chain.

NPC shopkeepers aren't spawning items out of thin air. They're connected to on-ledger AMMs — the same constant-product mechanism as the XRPL DEX — so resource pricing is dynamic, real-time, and driven by actual supply and demand. Flood the market with iron and the price drops. Corner the moonpetal supply and watch essence prices climb.

{# ── Real Ownership ── #}

Real Ownership of In-Game Assets

Every item of value in the game is a real on-chain asset. Your enchanted longsword is an NFT. Both your stack of iron ingots and your game gold are issued currencies on the XRP Ledger. From beta, these can be exported to the XRPL and traded or sold outside the game.

While you're playing, assets sit in a game-controlled bridge wallet. From beta onwards, you'll be able to import and export them to your own wallet at will. They're yours. During pre-alpha and alpha, the game operates in closed-loop mode while we balance the economy.

This isn't a token bolted onto a game. The XRPL is the ledger, and the economic loop is complete: gold earned through questing becomes resources, resources become crafted items, items get traded, and gold circulates back into the world for others to earn. The full circle.

{# ── AI Integration ── #}

AI-Driven Emergent Gameplay

NPCs remember previous interactions with your character. Relationships matter.

A persistent bard with high charisma and a willingness to give gifts — to build a genuine relationship with a boss mob over time — might just be gifted the item that everyone else tries to kill the boss for. Not because the game has a "friendship meter" — because the AI driving that mob remembers who you are, remembers what you've done, and decides you've earned it.

Higher-level mobs use the same combat system as players. They have abilities, commands, races, and classes — direct analogues of player characters. An AI decides what commands to call, drawing on embedding-based memory of what worked in past fights and what didn't. They actively learn. They experiment with new tactics. The goblin shaman that got destroyed by fire magic last time might open with a resistance spell next time. The bandit captain who learned that stunning the healer first wins fights will keep doing it.

Players can group with AI-driven characters — as equals, as retainers, as pets. These companions take high-level direction from the player but act largely independently, making their own tactical decisions in combat, responding to the world around them.

{# ── Procedural World ── #}

A World That Stays Fresh

Yes, some of the paths can be memorised. The main roads, the towns, the landmarks — these are fixed, and learning the world is part of the experience.

But a large part of the world is generated procedurally. The dungeon you cleared yesterday won't have the same layout today. You need to freshly explore the way to the boss mob every time you go to kill it. No walkthrough, no memorised path, no autopilot. Every expedition is a real expedition.

Combined with AI-driven mobs that learn and adapt, this means that even veteran players face genuine uncertainty. The world doesn't get solved. It stays dangerous, surprising, and alive.

{# ── The Feel ── #}

The Feel

At its core, Full Circle MUD is chasing a feeling. The feeling of logging into a text world at midnight on a school night, heart pounding because you're about to attempt a dungeon run you've been planning all week with people you've never met in person but trust with your virtual life.

That feeling — of genuine risk, genuine reward, genuine community — is what the old MUDs had. Full Circle MUD aims to recreate it, then add what those games could never offer: an economy built on the blockchain, NPCs that remember your name, and a world that never stops surprising you.
